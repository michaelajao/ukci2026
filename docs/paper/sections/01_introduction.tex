\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
% =============================================================================

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the vulnerability of national health systems
to rapid, spatially uneven surges in critical-care demand. In England,
sustained demand for adult critical-care beds and mechanical ventilation
during successive waves required emergency capacity expansion and
inter-region patient transfers. Decisions of this scale are made under
deep uncertainty: planners must balance the cost of expanding surge
capacity against the risk of unmet demand, while keeping patient-transfer
burden manageable across the seven NHS England regions.
The computational intelligence community has produced strong tools for
both halves of this problem --- epidemic and hospital-demand
forecasting~\cite{gao2021stan,gao2023hoist,qian2025physics} and robust
facility-location and resource allocation under
uncertainty~\cite{eriskin2022robust,wan2023two,shamseddin2025optimization}
--- but few works connect the two into a single decision-focused pipeline
tailored to a national health system. The present work occupies the
\emph{temporal, per-region} corner of the forecasting design space and
the \emph{multi-method comparative} corner of the allocation design
space; the spatiotemporal-graph alternatives surveyed in
Section~\ref{sec:related_work} are explicitly deferred to future work.

Existing forecasting models, including physics-informed neural networks
(PINNs)~\cite{ajao2025hybrid,qian2025physics} and graph-based
architectures such as STAN~\cite{gao2021stan} and HOIST~\cite{gao2023hoist},
are typically optimised for statistical accuracy alone. Yet for
critical-care planning the operational cost of forecast errors is
asymmetric: underestimating demand by ten beds risks patients without
ventilators, while overestimating wastes prepared capacity. Standard
squared-error or absolute-error objectives treat these symmetrically.
Conversely, most optimisation formulations for pandemic capacity planning
assume demand is deterministic or drawn from off-the-shelf statistical
forecasts~\cite{shamseddin2025optimization,eriskin2022robust}, leaving
unexploited the dynamics-aware information that physics-informed
forecasters can provide. Recent work begins to close this gap:
Liu and Cao~\cite{liu2026dynamic} couple spatiotemporal graph attention
with multi-objective optimisation for vaccine and
non-pharmaceutical-intervention allocation, and Shams Eddin and El
Hajj~\cite{shamseddin2025optimization} integrate SEIRD-driven demand into
robust hospital-bed scheduling for a single US region. To our knowledge no
published work combines (a) physics-informed regional epidemic forecasting,
(b) decision-aware training that explicitly penalises operationally costly
forecast errors, and (c) metaheuristic robust optimisation for NHS England
critical-care surge capacity.

We close that gap with a forecast-to-decision pipeline organised in four
phases. \emph{Phase A} is a per-region PINN-SEIRD module that produces
multi-horizon quantile forecasts of mechanical-ventilation bed occupancy.
The PINN is first pre-trained to satisfy the refined $SEI_aI_sHCRD$ ODE
system, producing twelve time-varying physics-informed features (eight
compartmental states and four transmission/clinical parameters) that are
then fed --- frozen --- into a temporal GRU head. The head emits the
$10$th, $50$th and $90$th predictive quantiles per horizon \emph{directly}
via a multi-quantile \emph{pinball loss} (decision-aware by construction,
since the $q^{(0.9)}$ component asymmetrically penalises under-prediction
nine-to-one), with no post-hoc Monte-Carlo sampling. A learnable
level-and-trend anchor skip-connects the most recent observation and
recent weekly slope to every horizon-quantile prediction, trading
short-horizon flexibility for long-horizon stability. \emph{Phase B}
discretises the quantile forecasts into three demand scenarios
(low/median/high) with probabilities $\pi = (0.20, 0.60, 0.20)$.
\emph{Phase C} solves a regional bed-surge allocation problem using
those scenarios, comparing a deterministic mixed-integer linear
programme, a tail-risk robust MILP, a Genetic Algorithm with
LP-slave decomposition, the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm
II~\cite{deb2002fast}, Simulated Annealing, and four closed-form
heuristic baselines. \emph{Phase D} reports operational metrics
(expected unmet, worst-case unmet, transfer burden, runtime) plus
sensitivity to the surge budget, the CVaR weight, and the mutual-aid
travel-time cap.

Our contributions are: (1) a forecast-to-decision pipeline reproducible
on open NHS data, with all four phases released as installable Python
console commands; (2) a \emph{decision-aware} multi-quantile pinball
training objective coupled with a level-and-trend anchor skip connection,
jointly responsible for a $19\%$ RMSE reduction over per-origin
re-fitted ARIMA at the operational $14$-day horizon and a four-fold
reduction in underestimation rate at $h = 28$; (3) a hybrid-regional
robust allocation model with explicit MILP/GA/NSGA-II/SA comparison,
revealing that the robust MILP achieves the lowest transfer burden in
the policy set ($4.6\times$ below population-proportional, at identical
expected and worst-case unmet) and that the metaheuristics match it
within a few percent at $20$--$115$~s runtime; (4) a refined
$SEI_aI_sHCRD$ compartmental model that separates the hospitalisation
ratio $\omega$ ($I_s\to H$) from the critical-care escalation proportion
$\phi$ ($H\to C$), correcting an over-loading of $\omega$ in earlier
work~\cite{ajao2025hybrid}; and (5) an NHS England case study spanning
five forecasters --- PinnGRU, ARIMA, per-region GRU, XGBoost, and
seasonal-naive(7) --- plus a three-axis sensitivity sweep over surge
budget, CVaR weight, and travel-time cap, quantifying when forecast
quality versus parameter choice dominates the operational outcome.
\Cref{fig:pipeline} summarises the four-phase pipeline.
\Cref{sec:related_work} reviews related work;
\Cref{sec:forecasting,sec:optimisation} describe the forecasting and
optimisation modules; \Cref{sec:case_study} presents the case study;
\Cref{sec:results,sec:discussion,sec:conclusion} report results, discuss
limitations, and conclude.
