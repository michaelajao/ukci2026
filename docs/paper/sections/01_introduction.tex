NHS critical-care surge planning during epidemic waves balances the
cost of pre-positioning capacity against the risk of unmet demand while
keeping inter-regional transfers manageable across the seven NHS England
regions. COVID-19 placed sustained pressure on adult
mechanical-ventilation capacity, making this a coupled forecasting and
allocation problem under regime shift. Existing forecasting work spans
statistical and recurrent baselines~\cite{ajao2024deep}, graph-based
epidemic and hospital-demand models~\cite{gao2021stan,gao2023hoist},
and physics-informed neural networks~\cite{raissi2019physics,ajao2025hybrid,qian2025physics};
interval and quantile evaluation is also standard in epidemic forecast
hubs~\cite{bracher2021evaluating}. These forecasters are usually
optimised for symmetric statistical accuracy, although ICU planning has
an asymmetric error cost: under-prediction risks stranded patients,
whereas over-prediction wastes prepared capacity.

On the allocation side, robust facility-location and health-resource
models handle demand uncertainty with MILP, two-stage optimisation, and
metaheuristics~\cite{eriskin2022robust,wan2023two,shamseddin2025optimization,deb2002fast}.
Bertsimas et al.~\cite{bertsimas2022where} couple DELPHI dynamics to a
bilinear MIP for vaccination-site location, while Luo and
Stellato~\cite{luo2024equitable} embed neural-ODE compartmental dynamics
inside a McCormick-relaxed facility-location model. Our setting differs
from Smart Predict-then-Optimize~\cite{elmachtoub2022smart}: the
forecaster is not trained through allocation regret, but its
multi-quantile pinball objective is cost-asymmetric and feeds a robust
optimisation layer. To our knowledge, no prior work combines
physics-informed NHS regional ICU-bed forecasting with cost-asymmetric
quantile training and an exact robust linear-programming allocation
across the seven NHS England regions.

This paper presents a forecast-to-decision pipeline
(\Cref{fig:pipeline}) organised in four phases. \emph{Phase~A}
pre-trains a per-region PINN on the $SEI_aI_sHCRD$ ODE system. Its
twelve outputs, comprising eight compartmental states and four
transmission and clinical parameters, are frozen and passed to a
temporal GRU head that emits the horizon-wise quantiles
$q^{(0.1)}$, $q^{(0.5)}$, $q^{(0.9)}$ trained directly with a
multi-quantile pinball loss. The loss is operationally aligned rather
than solver-coupled: the $q^{(0.9)}$ branch penalises under-prediction
at a nine-to-one ratio. A level-and-trend anchor
skip-connects the most recent observation and its weekly slope to
every quantile prediction. \emph{Phase~B} discretises these
quantiles into three scenarios (low, median, and high) with weights
$\pi=(0.2, 0.6, 0.2)$. \emph{Phase~C} solves the resulting
allocation exactly as a linear program (deterministic and
tail-risk-robust) via PuLP$+$CBC, benchmarked against three
closed-form heuristic baselines on a shared LP-slave oracle.
\emph{Phase~D} reports operational
metrics and a sensitivity analysis over the forecaster, the budget
$B$, the transfer penalty $\lambda_3$, and the surge threshold
$\tau$.

The main contributions are as follows:
(1)~\textbf{Cost-asymmetric forecaster.} The combination of a
multi-quantile pinball loss and a level-and-trend anchor reduces
$h{=}14$ RMSE by $19\%$ relative to refitted ARIMA and $37\%$ relative
to a per-region GRU; paired origin-level tests and ablations isolate
where this gain holds and where ARIMA remains stronger.
(2)~\textbf{Robust LP allocation.} A hybrid-regional formulation
attains the lowest transfer burden among budget-exhausting policies at
the reported operating point, $4.6\times$ below the population-
proportional baseline under tied coverage; this is interpreted as a
binding-budget routing effect rather than an unmet-demand reduction.
(3)~\textbf{$SEI_aI_sHCRD$ clinical split.} The proposed model
separates the hospitalisation ratio $\omega$ ($I_s\to H$) from the
critical-care escalation rate $\phi$ ($H\to C$), correcting the
overloaded $\omega$ used in~\cite{ajao2025hybrid}. The remainder of the paper is organised as follows.
\Cref{sec:forecasting,sec:optimisation,sec:case_study} describe the
forecasting and optimisation modules and the case study;
\Cref{sec:results} reports results; and \Cref{sec:conclusion}
discusses limitations and concludes.
