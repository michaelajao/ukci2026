\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
% =============================================================================

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the vulnerability of national health systems
to rapid, spatially uneven surges in critical-care demand. In England,
sustained demand for adult critical-care beds and mechanical ventilation
during successive waves required emergency capacity expansion, inter-trust
patient transfers, and Nightingale-style temporary hospitals. Decisions of
this scale are made under deep uncertainty: planners must balance the cost
of opening or expanding surge capacity against the risk of unmet demand,
while remaining sensitive to patient-transfer burden and regional equity.
The computational intelligence community has produced strong tools for
both halves of this problem --- epidemic and hospital-demand
forecasting~\cite{gao2021stan,gao2023hoist,qian2025physics} and robust
facility-location and resource allocation under
uncertainty~\cite{eriskin2022robust,wan2023two,shamseddin2025optimization}
--- but few works connect the two into a single decision-focused pipeline
tailored to a national health system. The present work occupies the
\emph{temporal, per-region} corner of the forecasting design space and
the \emph{multi-method comparative} corner of the allocation design
space; the spatiotemporal-graph alternatives surveyed in
Section~\ref{sec:related_work} are explicitly deferred to future work.

Existing forecasting models, including physics-informed neural networks
(PINNs)~\cite{ajao2025hybrid,qian2025physics} and graph-based
architectures such as STAN~\cite{gao2021stan} and HOIST~\cite{gao2023hoist},
are typically optimised for statistical accuracy alone. Yet for
critical-care planning the operational cost of forecast errors is
asymmetric: underestimating demand by ten beds risks patients without
ventilators, while overestimating wastes prepared capacity. Standard
squared-error or absolute-error objectives treat these symmetrically.
Conversely, most optimisation formulations for pandemic capacity planning
assume demand is deterministic or drawn from off-the-shelf statistical
forecasts~\cite{shamseddin2025optimization,eriskin2022robust}, leaving
unexploited the dynamics-aware information that physics-informed
forecasters can provide. Recent work begins to close this gap:
Liu and Cao~\cite{liu2026dynamic} couple spatiotemporal graph attention
with multi-objective optimisation for vaccine and
non-pharmaceutical-intervention allocation, and Shams Eddin and El
Hajj~\cite{shamseddin2025optimization} integrate SEIRD-driven demand into
robust hospital-bed scheduling for a single US region. To our knowledge no
published work combines (a) physics-informed regional epidemic forecasting,
(b) decision-aware training that explicitly penalises operationally costly
forecast errors, and (c) metaheuristic robust optimisation for NHS England
critical-care surge capacity.

We close that gap with a four-phase forecast-to-decision pipeline. A
per-region PINN-SEIRD module produces multi-horizon point and quantile
forecasts of mechanical-ventilation bed occupancy. The PINN is first
pre-trained to satisfy the refined $SEI_aI_sHCRD$ ODE system, producing
twelve time-varying physics-informed features (eight compartmental states
and four transmission/clinical parameters) that are then fed --- frozen ---
into a temporal GRU head. The GRU emits the $10$th, $50$th and $90$th
predictive quantiles per horizon \emph{directly} via a multi-quantile
\emph{pinball loss} (decision-aware by construction, since the $q^{0.9}$
component asymmetrically penalises under-prediction nine-to-one), with no
post-hoc Monte-Carlo sampling. The head augments the GRU output with a
learnable level-and-trend anchor that residual-connects the most recent
observation and recent weekly slope to every horizon-quantile prediction,
trading short-horizon flexibility for long-horizon stability. Forecast
quantiles are discretised into demand scenarios that drive a robust
multi-objective location-allocation model, solved exactly via mixed-integer
linear programming (MILP) at NHS regional scale, and via metaheuristics ---
a Genetic Algorithm (GA), the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II
(NSGA-II)~\cite{deb2002fast}, and Simulated Annealing --- as a comparison
of decision-quality versus runtime trade-offs.

Our contributions are: (1) a four-phase forecast-to-decision pipeline
reproducible on open NHS data; (2) a \emph{decision-aware} multi-quantile
pinball training objective coupled with a level-and-trend anchor skip
connection, jointly responsible for the proposed forecaster's $19\%$
improvement over ARIMA at the $14$-day operational horizon and four-fold
lower underestimation rate at $h=28$; (3) a robust multi-objective
allocation model balancing infrastructure cost, unmet demand, transfer
burden, and equity, with explicit MILP/GA/NSGA-II/SA comparison;
(4) a refined $SEI_aI_sHCRD$ compartmental model that separates the
hospitalisation ratio $\omega$ ($I_s\to H$) from the critical-care
escalation proportion $\phi$ ($H\to C$), correcting an over-loading of
$\omega$ in earlier work~\cite{ajao2025hybrid}; and (5) an NHS England
case study evaluating allocation policies against operational, equity,
and robustness metrics under PinnGRU, ARIMA, and seasonal-naive forecast
inputs, quantifying the value of decision-aware forecasting downstream.
\Cref{sec:related_work} reviews related work;
\Cref{sec:forecasting,sec:optimisation} describe the forecasting and
optimisation modules; \Cref{sec:case_study} presents the case study;
\Cref{sec:results,sec:discussion,sec:conclusion} report results, discuss
limitations, and conclude.
