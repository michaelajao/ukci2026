\section{Results and Discussion}\label{sec:results}
% =============================================================================

All results use the Omicron test split (1~Dec~2021--31~Aug~2022).

\subsection{Forecasting Accuracy}\label{ssec:e1}

\Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} reports per-horizon RMSE and the
underestimation rate at $h{=}28$ for PinnGRU against four baselines, all
sharing the same $32$-origin rolling-origin protocol. PinnGRU wins
$h{=}7$ and $h{=}14$; at the operational $14$-day horizon it cuts RMSE
by $19\%$ over ARIMA ($14.46$ vs $17.80$) and by $37\%$ over the
per-region GRU ($14.46$ vs $22.92$). Origin-bootstrap intervals in
\Cref{fig:forecast_uncertainty} show that the paired $h{=}14$ RMSE
gain is robust to rolling-origin uncertainty: PinnGRU--ARIMA is
$-3.88$ MV beds (95\% CI $[-5.97,-1.74]$, Wilcoxon $p{=}0.003$),
and PinnGRU--GRU is $-6.95$ MV beds (95\% CI $[-10.21,-4.33]$,
$p{<}10^{-6}$). The same paired analysis makes the long-horizon trade-off
explicit: ARIMA is significantly better than PinnGRU at $h{=}28$
($+5.53$ MV beds, 95\% CI $[1.96,9.05]$, $p{=}0.006$), while PinnGRU
continues to dominate the neural GRU baseline. Thus the proposed model is
best read as a short-to-medium horizon operational forecaster, not a
uniform point-accuracy winner across all horizons.

The bottom block of \Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} ablates the three
load-bearing components. \textbf{(i)} Replacing the pinball loss with
MSE on the median channel inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE by $29\%$ and drops
PinnGRU below several classical baselines; the cost-asymmetric loss
improves the RMSE trade-off even though the MSE ablation has the lowest
marginal $h{=}28$ underestimation rate. \textbf{(ii)} Zeroing the four PINN
parameter features inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE almost two-fold
($14.46\!\to\!27.25$), the largest regression in the table.
\textbf{(iii)} Skipping PINN pre-training inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE by
$19\%$ ($14.46\!\to\!17.27$), confirming that the physics prior, not
capacity, drives the gain over the per-region GRU baseline. Per-component
anchor ablations (omitted for space) show the level anchor improves
$h{=}7$ marginally but degrades $h{=}28$ by $17\%$ when removed, and
the trend anchor degrades both ends; the anchor pair trades short-
horizon flexibility for long-horizon stability.

\subsection{Allocation Performance Across Solvers}
\label{ssec:e2_e3}

\Cref{tab:allocation} compares all eight policies on the seven-region
problem with $B = 213$~beds ($20\%$ of total baseline), four-hour
mutual-aid cap, and PinnGRU $q^{(0.1)}\!/q^{(0.5)}\!/q^{(0.9)}$
forecasts at the first Omicron-period origin. Every policy's chosen
allocation is re-evaluated by the same LP slave on the full three-
scenario set so $E[u]$ and $u^{\text{worst}}$ are reported on equal
footing.

\textbf{(i)} The \emph{robust MILP} achieves the lowest transfer
burden in the table ($672$~bed-km, $4.6\times$ below
population-proportional and $1.7\times$ below greedy shortage-first),
but this is a tie-break among policies with identical rounded $E[u]$
and $u^{\text{worst}}$, not a coverage improvement. \textbf{(ii)} The
\emph{deterministic MILP} spends only $51$ of the $213$ available beds
and pays $\sim\!3\times$ higher $E[u]$ and $u^{\text{worst}}$,
quantifying the value of $\pi_s$-weighted expectation over median-only
optimisation. \textbf{(iii)} GA and SA match the robust MILP on the
coverage metrics and come close on transfer ($685$--$830$~bed-km), but
at substantially longer runtimes. The robust MILP wins on \emph{routing},
not coverage: once the budget binds, robust-feasible policies
differentiate only on transfer burden.

Inspecting the regional structure behind \Cref{tab:allocation}, the
deterministic MILP allocates surge only into Midlands and South East
(median demand exceeds baseline there); budget-exhausting policies all
cover those two and differ markedly on the remaining five, with the
robust MILP and GA finding compromise allocations with non-trivial
North~West and London surge while population-proportional spreads
mechanically and greedy concentrates on top-demand regions.
The NSGA-II representative point is the L2-normalised ideal point on
the Pareto front, so it deliberately selects a lower-surge elbow rather
than matching the GA/robust-MILP coverage point.

\subsection{Forecast-Quality and Parameter Sensitivity}
\label{ssec:e5_e6}

\paragraph{Forecast-quality robustness.}
Substituting each forecaster into the robust MILP at the
$29$~Dec~$2021$ origin: PinnGRU's $q^{(0.9)}$ peak sum
($1421$~beds$\,\to\,212.7$ surge), XGBoost ($1105\,\to\,197.5$), GRU
($973\,\to\,135.5$), ARIMA ($875\,\to\,73.2$), and seasonal-naive($7$)
($745\,\to\,0$); all exceed the realised peak ($797$) except
seasonal-naive, which under-forecasts the peak by $52$~beds. Realised
unmet is zero throughout, including the oracle, so this origin cannot
separate policies on realised coverage. Forecast quality buys
\emph{efficiency} at the $q^{(0.9)}$ margin, not realised coverage in
this observed Omicron instance; a wave exceeding the Delta baseline is
needed to stress the under-provisioning metric directly.

Repeating the exact and closed-form policies over all $32$ Omicron origins
supports the same interpretation. Robust MILP, population-proportional, and
demand-proportional allocations have the same mean expected unmet to one
decimal place ($2.9$), but the robust MILP reduces mean transfer burden to
$76$~bed-km compared with $406$ and $337$~bed-km for the two proportional
baselines. The all-origin check therefore confirms a routing-efficiency gain
under tied coverage rather than a realised-unmet separation.

\paragraph{Parameter sensitivity.}
\Cref{tab:e6} sweeps each parameter around the operating point. The
budget $B$ dominates: $B/\sum_r C_r$ from $0.20$ to $0.10$ doubles
$E[u]$ and triples $u^{\text{worst}}$; $0.30$ cuts $E[u]$ to
$\sim\!1/4$. $\lambda_3$ is \emph{non-binding} at $B = 0.20$ because
the expectation-minimising solution already clears the high tail; a
tighter budget ($\leq\!15\%$) would re-engage it, so the tail-risk
term is best read as a diagnostic for when tail risk binds rather
than a defect when it does not. The travel cap $\tau\!=\!120$~min cuts
transfer burden by $34\%$ at a $3\%$ rise in $E[u]$;
$\tau\!\geq\!240$~min is slack.
