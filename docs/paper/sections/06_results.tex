\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
% Target length: ~2.25 pages (~1000-1100 words)
% =============================================================================

We report results for five experiments: forecasting accuracy (E1),
regional allocation comparing exact MILP, metaheuristics, and naive
baselines (E2--E3), three-objective Pareto-front analysis (E4),
robustness to forecast quality (E5), and sensitivity to surge budget,
CVaR weight, and travel-time cap (E6). All results use the chronological
test split ($1$~Dec~$2021$ -- $31$~Aug~$2022$).

\subsection{Forecasting Accuracy (E1)}\label{ssec:e1}

\Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} reports root mean squared error (RMSE) by
horizon and the underestimation rate at the operational $28$-day horizon
for the proposed PinnGRU against four baselines: a seasonal-naive(7) floor,
a per-region SARIMAX with AIC-selected order refit at every origin, a
direct multi-step XGBoost on lag features (also refit per origin), and a
non-physics univariate GRU baseline trained once on the full
train+validation split. All models share the same rolling-origin protocol
($32$ origins, $7$-day stride) over the Omicron test period.

The proposed model achieves the best $h=7$ and $h=14$ RMSE in the table
($11.02$ and $14.46$ respectively); at $h=14$ the latter is a $19\%$
reduction over ARIMA ($17.80$) at the operational two-week surge-planning
horizon. Underestimation rate at $h=28$ is $10.3\%$ for PinnGRU versus
$44.2\%$ for ARIMA --- a four-fold reduction --- and is consistent with the
asymmetric design of the multi-quantile pinball loss in \Cref{eq:pinball}.
ARIMA edges PinnGRU on long-horizon ($h=21$, $h=28$) RMSE; this reflects
its per-origin refit advantage on regime breaks, which PinnGRU \emph{could}
match via online adaptation but at the cost of the one-shot training
simplicity that makes the proposed model attractive operationally.

The bottom block of \Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} ablates the five additive
components of the proposed architecture and isolates the contribution of
each. Three findings are noteworthy. First, replacing the multi-quantile
pinball loss with a mean-squared-error objective on the median channel
alone (\emph{w/o decision-aware loss}) inflates $h=14$ RMSE by $29\%$
($14.46 \to 18.70$) and pushes accuracy below several classical baselines,
confirming that the pinball loss is the proximate cause of the proposed
model's accuracy advantage --- not merely the architecture. Second, the
PINN parameter features $(\beta_r, \gamma_{c,r}, \delta_{c,r}, \eta_r)$
are not auxiliary: replacing them with zeros while keeping the eight
compartmental state features inflates $h=14$ RMSE almost two-fold
($14.46 \to 27.25$), the largest single regression in the table. The
time-varying parameters \emph{themselves} carry information about how
fast the dynamics are evolving (supplementary), independently of
where in the wave the region currently sits. Third, the level and trend
anchors trade short-horizon flexibility for long-horizon stability:
removing the level anchor improves the $h=7$ RMSE marginally ($11.02 \to
10.23$) but collapses $h=28$ accuracy ($34.63 \to 40.51$), and removing
the trend anchor degrades both ends. All five components are required for
the full operating point.

\subsection{Allocation: MILP, Metaheuristics, and Naive Baselines (E2--E3)}
\label{ssec:e2_e3}

\Cref{tab:allocation} compares all nine allocation policies on the
seven-region NHS England regional problem, with a surge budget of
$B = 213$ beds ($20\%$ of total baseline capacity), four-hour mutual-aid
transfer cap, and the three-scenario set of PinnGRU $q^{(0.1)},
q^{(0.5)}, q^{(0.9)}$ forecasts at the first Omicron-period decision
origin. Every policy's chosen allocation is re-evaluated by the same LP
slave on the full three-scenario set, so expected and worst-case
shortage are reported on equal footing.

Three findings stand out. First, the \emph{robust MILP} (CVaR
$\lambda_3 = 1$) achieves the best transfer burden in the table
($672$ bed-km) at the same expected unmet ($29$ beds) and worst-case
unmet ($145$ beds) achieved by every other budget-exhausting policy. It
delivers a $4.6\times$ reduction in transfer burden versus the
population-proportional baseline ($3120$ bed-km) and a $1.7\times$
reduction versus greedy shortage-first ($1140$~bed-km), at no cost in
coverage. Second, the \emph{deterministic MILP} --- which optimises the
median scenario only --- chooses to spend only $51$ of the $213$
available surge beds, accepting an expected unmet of $83$ and a worst-
case unmet of $416$ beds. The gap between deterministic and robust thus
quantifies the \emph{value of the robust solution}: spending the
additional $162$ surge beds reduces expected unmet by $65\%$ and worst-
case unmet by $65\%$, an effect invisible to any single-scenario
optimiser. Third, the metaheuristics (GA, NSGA-II representative point,
SA) all match the robust MILP on expected and worst-case unmet, with
transfer burdens between $685$ and $830$ bed-km --- comparable to the
exact robust solution but at runtimes of $20$--$115$~s versus the MILP's
$<\!0.1$~s.

\Cref{fig:allocation_heatmap} reveals the regional structure behind the
aggregate numbers in \Cref{tab:allocation}. Status quo and deterministic
MILP allocate zero beds outside Midlands and South East. The
budget-exhausting policies all surge significantly into Midlands and
South East (which dominate forecast peak load) but differ markedly on
the remaining five regions: population-proportional spreads beds
mechanically by population; greedy shortage-first concentrates them in
the two highest-demand regions; the robust MILP and GA find compromise
allocations that put non-trivial surge in North West and London while
keeping Midlands and South East covered. The Simulated Annealing
solution is qualitatively similar to the GA solution but with higher
transfer burden, consistent with its more local search behaviour.

\subsection{Pareto Front Analysis (E4)}\label{ssec:e4}

NSGA-II evaluates the three-objective formulation \eqref{eq:nsga2_f1}--
\eqref{eq:nsga2_f3} (total surge beds, expected unmet, transfer burden)
without scalarisation, returning a Pareto front of $50$ non-dominated
solutions across $40$ generations. \Cref{fig:nsga2_pareto} projects this
front onto the two two-dimensional slices that matter most for capacity
planning. The left panel reveals a sharp surge--unmet knee around
$200$~beds: below this point, every additional bed pays for itself in
substantial expected-unmet reduction; above it, returns diminish quickly
as transfer burden dominates the marginal cost. The right panel
illustrates the transfer--unmet trade-off at fixed budget: even at the
same total surge spend, different inter-regional allocations differ by
an order of magnitude in transfer burden, with the lowest transfer
burden achieved jointly by the robust MILP and the Genetic Algorithm.
The NSGA-II representative point lies precisely at the curvature elbow
of the surge--unmet front, formalising the intuition that two-thirds of
the unmet reduction is captured by the first half of the budget.

\subsection{Robustness and Sensitivity (E5--E6)}\label{ssec:e5_e6}

We report sensitivity along four axes: the upstream forecaster
(\Cref{tab:e5}), the surge budget $B$, the CVaR weight $\lambda_3$, and
the travel-time cap $\tau$ (\Cref{tab:e6}). All sweeps fix the first
Omicron decision origin (29~Dec~2021) and the regional formulation of
\Cref{sec:optimisation}.

\paragraph{E5 — Forecast-quality robustness.}
\Cref{tab:e5} substitutes the PinnGRU forecaster with each baseline in
the same robust MILP and compares the chosen surge investment against
the realised peak demand at that origin. Three observations follow.
First, every model except seasonal-naive(7) drives an over-provisioning
allocation: the sum of $q^{(0.9)}$ peak demands across the seven regions
exceeds the realised peak ($797$ beds) by a factor of $1.1\times$
(ARIMA) up to $1.8\times$ (PinnGRU). Second, this over-conservatism is
the operationally important behaviour, not a defect: the same baseline
capacity (Delta-peak $\times 1.05$) is generous relative to the realised
Omicron peak, so under-provisioning is the danger, not over-provisioning.
Seasonal-naive's $q^{(0.9)}$-equivalent ($745$~beds) falls $52$~beds
\emph{below} the realised peak; PinnGRU's $1421$ has the largest margin
against an unforeseen tail. Third, realised unmet is zero across the
board, including the oracle ($y_\text{true}$ as demand), confirming
that the regional Delta-peak baseline alone covered the realised
Omicron peak --- the surge mechanism's value lies in pre-positioning
capacity against forecast tails, not in clearing the realised path.

\paragraph{E6 — Sensitivity to $B$, $\lambda_3$, and $\tau$.}
\Cref{tab:e6} sweeps the three free parameters one at a time around the
operating point of \Cref{tab:allocation}. The budget $B$ is the
dominant control: a five-point cut from $B/\sum_r C_r = 0.20$ to $0.10$
doubles expected unmet ($29 \to 61$) and triples worst-case unmet
($145 \to 306$); a five-point increase to $0.30$ cuts expected unmet
to a quarter of its operating-point value ($29 \to 7.7$). The CVaR
weight $\lambda_3$ is \emph{not} binding at $B = 0.20$: every value in
$\{0, 0.5, 1, 2, 4\}$ yields the same allocation, because at this
budget the expectation-minimising solution already saturates capacity
across all three scenarios. (The deterministic--robust gap in
\Cref{tab:allocation} is therefore best read as the gap between
optimising the median scenario alone vs.\ optimising the
$\pi_s$-weighted expectation; the CVaR machinery contributes the
guarantee that the median solution remains optimal even under tail
re-weighting, but not a different solution.) The travel-time cap
$\tau$ binds only at the tight end: $\tau = 120$~min isolates a few
distant region pairs (notably London $\leftrightarrow$ South West,
North~West $\leftrightarrow$ South~East), buying a $34\%$ cut in
transfer burden ($672 \to 444$~bed-km) at a $3\%$ rise in expected
unmet ($29.0 \to 29.9$). For $\tau \geq 240$~min the cap is slack and
solutions are identical.
