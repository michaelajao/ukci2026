\section{Results and Discussion}\label{sec:results}

All results use the Omicron test split (1~Dec~2021--31~Aug~2022).

\subsection{Forecasting Accuracy}\label{ssec:e1}

\Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} reports per-horizon RMSE and the
underestimation rate at $h{=}28$ for PinnGRU against four baselines, all
sharing the same $32$-origin rolling-origin protocol. PinnGRU wins
$h{=}7$ and $h{=}14$; at the operational $14$-day horizon it cuts RMSE
by $19\%$ over ARIMA ($14.46$ vs $17.80$). The underestimation rate at
$h{=}28$ falls from ARIMA's $44.2\%$ to $10.3\%$, a four-fold
reduction consistent with the asymmetric design of the multi-quantile
pinball loss (\Cref{eq:pinball}). ARIMA edges PinnGRU at $h{=}21,28$
through its per-origin refit, a parity PinnGRU could close via online
adaptation at the cost of its one-shot operational simplicity. The
per-region forecast trajectories in \Cref{fig:forecast_panel} make
this visible: PinnGRU tracks the Omicron descent in every region with
shorter under-prediction episodes than the baselines.

The bottom block of \Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} ablates the five
architectural components. \textbf{(i)} Replacing the pinball loss with
MSE on the median channel inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE by $29\%$ and drops
PinnGRU below several classical baselines; the loss, not the
architecture, drives the gain. \textbf{(ii)} Zeroing the four PINN
parameter features inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE almost two-fold
($14.46\!\to\!27.25$), the largest regression in the table.
\textbf{(iii)} The level and trend anchors trade short-horizon
flexibility for long-horizon stability. All five components are
required.

\subsection{Allocation: MILP, Metaheuristics, and Baselines}
\label{ssec:e2_e3}

\Cref{tab:allocation} compares all eight policies on the seven-region
problem with $B = 213$~beds ($20\%$ of total baseline), four-hour
mutual-aid cap, and PinnGRU $q^{(0.1)}\!/q^{(0.5)}\!/q^{(0.9)}$
forecasts at the first Omicron-period origin. Every policy's chosen
allocation is re-evaluated by the same LP slave on the full three-
scenario set so $E[u]$ and $u^{\text{worst}}$ are reported on equal
footing.

\textbf{(i)} The \emph{robust MILP} achieves the lowest transfer
burden in the table ($672$~bed-km, $4.6\times$ below
population-proportional and $1.7\times$ below greedy shortage-first)
at the same $E[u]$ and $u^{\text{worst}}$ as every budget-exhausting
policy. \textbf{(ii)} The \emph{deterministic MILP} spends only $51$
of the $213$ available beds and pays $\sim\!3\times$ higher $E[u]$
and $u^{\text{worst}}$, quantifying the value of $\pi_s$-weighted
expectation over median-only optimisation. \textbf{(iii)} The
metaheuristics match the robust MILP on coverage
($685$--$830$~bed-km transfer) at $500\text{--}3000\times$ slower
runtime. The robust MILP wins on \emph{routing}, not coverage: once
the budget binds, robust-feasible policies differentiate only on
transfer burden.

Inspecting the regional structure behind \Cref{tab:allocation}, the
deterministic MILP allocates surge only into Midlands and South East
(median demand exceeds baseline there); budget-exhausting policies all
cover those two and differ markedly on the remaining five, with the
robust MILP and GA finding compromise allocations with non-trivial
North~West and London surge while population-proportional spreads
mechanically and greedy concentrates on top-demand regions.

\subsection{Pareto Front}\label{ssec:e4}

NSGA-II returns $50$ non-dominated solutions over $40$ generations on
the three-objective formulation~\eqref{eq:nsga2_obj}; the two key
slices in \Cref{fig:nsga2_pareto} show a sharp surge--unmet knee at
$\sim\!200$~beds (below which each marginal bed reduces expected unmet
demand and above which transfer burden dominates) and an
order-of-magnitude spread in transfer burden at fixed total surge,
with the lowest jointly achieved by the robust MILP and GA. The
representative point lies at the curvature elbow, formalising that
two-thirds of the expected-unmet reduction is captured by the first
half of the budget.

\subsection{Forecaster and Parameter Sensitivity}
\label{ssec:e5_e6}

\paragraph{Forecast-quality robustness.}
Substituting each forecaster into the robust MILP at the
$29$~Dec~$2021$ origin: PinnGRU's $q^{(0.9)}$ peak sum
($1421$~beds$\,\to\,212.7$ surge), XGBoost ($1105\,\to\,197.5$), GRU
($973\,\to\,135.5$), ARIMA ($875\,\to\,73.2$), and seasonal-naive($7$)
($745\,\to\,0$); all exceed the realised peak ($797$) except
seasonal-naive, the only baseline that would under-provision (by
$52$~beds). Realised unmet is zero throughout including the oracle: at
the Delta-peak baseline, under-provisioning is the danger and a
conservative $q^{(0.9)}$ is operationally right. Forecast quality
buys \emph{efficiency} at the $q^{(0.9)}$ margin, not coverage; the
same margin would become the safety bound in a future wave exceeding
the Delta ceiling.

\paragraph{Sensitivity to $B$, $\lambda_3$, $\tau$.}
\Cref{tab:e6} sweeps each parameter around the operating point. The
budget $B$ dominates: $B/\sum_r C_r$ from $0.20$ to $0.10$ doubles
$E[u]$ and triples $u^{\text{worst}}$; $0.30$ cuts $E[u]$ to
$\sim\!1/4$. $\lambda_3$ is \emph{non-binding} at $B = 0.20$ because
the expectation-minimising solution already clears the high tail; a
tighter budget ($\leq\!15\%$) would re-engage it, so the tail-risk
term is best read as a diagnostic for when tail risk binds rather
than a defect when it does not. The travel cap $\tau\!=\!120$~min cuts
transfer burden by $34\%$ at a $3\%$ rise in $E[u]$;
$\tau\!\geq\!240$~min is slack.
