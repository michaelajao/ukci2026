\section{Results and Discussion}\label{sec:results}
% =============================================================================

All results use the Omicron test split (1~Dec~2021--31~Aug~2022).

\subsection{Forecasting Accuracy}\label{ssec:e1}

\Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} reports per-horizon RMSE and the
$h{=}28$ underestimation rate for PinnGRU (the frozen PINN encoder of
\Cref{ssec:pinn} with the GRU head of \Cref{ssec:temporal}) against
four baselines under a common $32$-origin rolling-origin protocol. PinnGRU wins $h{=}7$ and
$h{=}14$, cutting the operational $14$-day RMSE by $19\%$ over ARIMA
and $37\%$ over the per-region GRU. Paired origin-level tests
(\Cref{fig:forecast_uncertainty}) confirm the $h{=}14$ gain over ARIMA
is significant ($-3.88$ MV beds, Wilcoxon $p{=}0.003$) and that ARIMA
is in turn significantly stronger at $h{=}28$ ($+5.53$ MV beds,
$p{=}0.006$). The proposed model is therefore best read as a
short-to-medium-horizon operational forecaster, not a uniform
point-accuracy winner across all horizons.

A second observation concerns the non-ARIMA models.
XGBoost's $h{=}28$ RMSE ($58.88$) is the worst absolute
number in the table, but its degradation from $h{=}14$ to $h{=}28$
(factor $2.57$) is comparable to that of the per-region GRU
($1.92\times$); both are lag-only learners bounded by the $28$-day
history window and cannot extrapolate outside the training distribution
into Omicron's altered case-to-hospitalisation regime. ARIMA's smaller
degradation ($1.57\times$) reflects its per-origin refit, which absorbs
the local trend at each test point. PinnGRU degrades by $2.39\times$
but its absolute $h{=}28$ RMSE ($34.63$) is much closer to ARIMA's
than to the other neural and tree baselines: the ODE-parameter features
it consumes encode time-varying transmission dynamics that pure-lag
inputs cannot, consistent with the ``w/o PINN params'' ablation
isolated next.

The bottom block of \Cref{tab:forecast_accuracy} ablates the three
load-bearing components. \textbf{(i)} Replacing the pinball loss with
MSE on the median channel inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE by $29\%$ and drops
PinnGRU below several classical baselines; the cost-asymmetric loss
improves the RMSE trade-off even though the MSE ablation has the lowest
marginal $h{=}28$ underestimation rate. \textbf{(ii)} Zeroing the four PINN
parameter features inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE almost two-fold
($14.46\!\to\!27.25$), the largest regression in the table.
\textbf{(iii)} Skipping PINN pre-training inflates $h{=}14$ RMSE by
$19\%$ ($14.46\!\to\!17.27$), confirming that the physics prior, not
capacity, drives the gain over the per-region GRU baseline.

\subsection{Allocation Performance Across Solvers}
\label{ssec:e2_e3}

\Cref{tab:allocation} compares the allocation policies on the
seven-region problem with $B = 213$~beds ($20\%$ of total baseline), four-hour
mutual-aid cap, and PinnGRU $q^{(0.1)}\!/q^{(0.5)}\!/q^{(0.9)}$
forecasts at the first Omicron-period origin. Every policy's chosen
allocation is re-scored by the common LP re-evaluation over the full
three-scenario set so $E[u]$ and $u^{\text{worst}}$ are reported on
equal footing.

\textbf{(i)} The \emph{robust LP} achieves the lowest transfer
burden in the table ($672$~bed-km, $4.6\times$ below
population-proportional and $1.7\times$ below greedy shortage-first),
but this is a tie-break among policies with identical rounded $E[u]$
and $u^{\text{worst}}$, not a coverage improvement. \textbf{(ii)} The
\emph{deterministic LP} spends only $51$ of the $213$ available beds
and pays $\sim\!3\times$ higher $E[u]$ and $u^{\text{worst}}$,
quantifying the value of $\pi_s$-weighted expectation over median-only
optimisation. \textbf{(iii)} The robust LP therefore wins on
\emph{routing} rather than coverage. Once the budget binds, every
budget-exhausting policy ties on $E[u]$ and $u^{\text{worst}}$ and
differs only on transfer burden.

Inspecting the regional structure behind \Cref{tab:allocation}
(\Cref{fig:alloc_budget}a), the deterministic LP allocates surge only into
Midlands and South East (median demand exceeds baseline there);
budget-exhausting policies all cover those two and differ markedly on
the remaining five, with the robust LP finding a compromise allocation
with non-trivial North~West and London surge while
population-proportional spreads mechanically and greedy concentrates on
top-demand regions.

\subsection{Forecast-Quality and Parameter Sensitivity}
\label{ssec:e5_e6}

\paragraph{Forecast-quality robustness.}
Substituting each forecaster into the robust LP at the
$29$~Dec~$2021$ origin: PinnGRU's $q^{(0.9)}$ peak sum
($1421$~beds$\,\to\,212.7$ surge), XGBoost ($1105\,\to\,197.5$), GRU
($973\,\to\,135.5$), ARIMA ($875\,\to\,73.2$), and seasonal-naive($7$)
($745\,\to\,0$); all exceed the realised peak ($797$) except
seasonal-naive, which under-forecasts the peak by $52$~beds. Realised
unmet is zero throughout, including the oracle, so this origin cannot
separate policies on realised coverage. Forecast quality buys
\emph{efficiency} at the $q^{(0.9)}$ margin, not realised coverage in
this observed Omicron instance; a wave exceeding the Delta baseline is
needed to stress the under-provisioning metric directly.

Across all $32$ Omicron origins the same pattern holds. Robust LP,
population-proportional, and demand-proportional allocations share a
mean expected unmet of $2.9$, but the robust LP cuts mean transfer
burden to $76$~bed-km versus $406$ and $337$~bed-km for the
proportional baselines. The proportional baselines fill the full
$212.7$-bed budget at every origin by construction, whereas the robust
LP scales surge to local demand pressure (mean total surge
$35.4$~beds, hitting the cap only at peak origins), so the
routing-efficiency gain holds across the full demand distribution, not
only at the peak.

\paragraph{Parameter sensitivity.}
A one-at-a-time sweep around the operating point isolates each control.
The budget $B$ dominates (\Cref{fig:alloc_budget}b): $B/\sum_r C_r$
from $0.20$ to $0.10$ doubles $E[u]$ and triples $u^{\text{worst}}$,
while $0.30$ cuts $E[u]$ to $\sim\!1/4$. $\lambda_3$ is
\emph{non-binding} at $B = 0.20$ (the expectation-minimising solution
already clears the high tail): only a tighter budget ($\leq\!15\%$)
re-engages it, so the tail-risk term is best read as a diagnostic for
when tail risk binds rather than a defect when it does not. The travel
cap $\tau\!=\!120$~min cuts transfer burden by $34\%$ at a $3\%$ rise
in $E[u]$; $\tau\!\geq\!240$~min is slack.
