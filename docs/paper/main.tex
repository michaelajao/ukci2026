% --- Abstract ----------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
Epidemic waves create rapid, spatially uneven pressure on critical-care
services, requiring health systems to decide where to expand surge capacity
and how to allocate patients under demand uncertainty. We propose a
forecast-to-decision optimisation framework for critical-care surge capacity
planning in NHS England. A regional physics-informed neural epidemic
forecasting module estimates multi-horizon demand using a refined
$SEI_aI_sHCRD$ compartmental model with distinct hospitalisation and
critical-care escalation parameters, trained with a decision-aware loss that
asymmetrically penalises forecast underestimation. Quantile forecasts are
discretised into demand scenarios that drive a robust multi-objective
location-allocation model balancing infrastructure cost, unmet demand,
patient-transfer burden, and equity, subject to capacity, budget, and
travel-time constraints. We compare exact mixed-integer linear programming,
scenario-based robust MILP, a Genetic Algorithm with linear-programming
slave, the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II, and Simulated
Annealing on NHS England COVID-19 hospital activity and critical-care
capacity data. Allocation policies are evaluated on unmet demand, coverage,
mean travel burden, worst-case shortage, and a regional shortage-equity
index. The framework links predictive modelling with prescriptive
optimisation to support transparent, robust, and equitable critical-care
surge planning.

\keywords{physics-informed neural networks, robust optimisation,
          metaheuristics, NSGA-II, critical-care planning,
          epidemic forecasting, NHS England}
\end{abstract}
